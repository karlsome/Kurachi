What management needs to understand

1) Synthetic leather / leather behavior

Leather-like materials are not perfectly elastic. They are fibrous or composite-like, and they do not recover uniformly after bending. This means they can keep a deformation memory after being wound for long periods, especially under compression on the inner wraps.

2) Urethane layer behavior

Urethane is flexible, but it is also viscoelastic. That means it can slowly deform under load, store strain, and recover imperfectly. In a roll, the inner side is compressed while the outer side is stretched. Over time, the inner compressed region can buckle or keep shape memory.

3) Release paper + adhesive behavior

Release paper is typically stiffer than the soft urethane surface, while adhesive behaves like a shear layer. This makes the laminate asymmetric. Because the layers do not all bend the same way, internal stress becomes unbalanced, which increases curl, set, and wrinkle risk.

4) When all layers are combined

The stack behaves like a multilayer composite. Each layer has different stiffness and recovery behavior. When rolled tightly, the innermost wraps see the highest compressive strain. After unrolling, the layers try to recover differently, and that mismatch can appear as wrinkles or waviness.

5) PSA cold-flow under sustained pressure

Pressure-sensitive adhesive (PSA) is specifically designed to conform and bond under light pressure — and that same property works against the roll. When wound tightly, the PSA layer is held under sustained compressive and shear force for hours or days. Even at room temperature and without any heat, PSA will cold-flow: it slowly migrates laterally and permanently deforms. Once displaced, the surrounding layers lose their original register. The adhesive cannot fully recover after unrolling, leaving a zone of uneven bonding and layer misalignment that appears as permanent waviness or localized adhesion failure. This is a PSA-specific mechanism on top of, and separate from, the bending-strain problem described above.

Three factors that make tight rolling worse

These factors are additive. Any one alone raises the risk — all three together can cause irreversible damage even at a core diameter that would otherwise be acceptable.

Storage time

Viscoelastic materials creep: they slowly deform permanently under sustained load. A roll stored for 3 months is far worse than one stored for 1 hour. Once creep sets in the deformation is irreversible. Rolls waiting between production steps in a warehouse are especially at risk because no one checks progress of the damage.

Winding tension

Every outer wrap acts like a clamp on the layers below it. High winding tension adds radial compressive pressure on top of the bending strain from the curved core. This radial pressure peaks at the innermost wraps and grows with each additional layer wound above. A material that could survive the core diameter at low tension may fail when winding tension is high.

Temperature

Warm storage significantly accelerates PSA cold-flow and polymer creep. A roll stored in a summer warehouse without air conditioning can degrade as quickly as one kept at room temperature for twice as long. Cold creates a different risk: the urethane and leather layers become stiffer and more brittle, making surface cracking more likely instead of soft wrinkles.

What the damage looks like — four defect modes

Different failure modes appear depending on which factor dominates. All are most severe at the innermost wraps near the core. Identifying the mode helps pinpoint the root cause.

Mode 1

Transverse curl ("banana set")

The unrolled sheet refuses to lie flat and springs back into the shape of the roll. The inner face was compressed and the outer face was stretched throughout storage. After weeks the material stores this curvature permanently. This is the most common complaint from downstream cutting and bonding steps, and is directly caused by the bending-strain mismatch.

Mode 2

Tunnel wrinkle (length-direction waves)

Wavelike ridges run along the length of the material — the machine direction. This is caused by lateral buckling under excessive compressive strain. The waves are often visible only once the material is stretched flat under tension and then released, making them easy to overlook during initial unrolling. Tunnel wrinkles are especially common near the edges of narrow rolls.

Mode 3

PSA edge creep / squeeze-out

Under sustained radial compression the PSA layer migrates toward the side edges over time. After unrolling the edges feel tacky or show visible adhesive build-up. Even if the PSA does not fully squeeze out, the migration creates uneven adhesive thickness. This leads to inconsistent bonding strength or liner delamination at the edges of finished product.

Mode 4

Inner-wrap surface crush

The decorative grain or texture of the urethane face can be permanently flattened at the innermost wraps, where compressive pressure is highest. The result is a dull, glossy, or compressed-looking patch compared to the undamaged remainder of the roll. This is typically confined to the first few wraps near the core and is not recoverable by any subsequent process.

Presentation-ready explanation

Technical message

Wrinkles are not caused by diameter alone. They occur because the laminate is a multilayer system with unequal stiffness and recovery. Tight rolling increases surface strain at the innermost wraps. When the material is later flattened, the layers recover unevenly and visible wrinkles can remain.

Management message

If the roll core is too small, product quality risk increases. A larger inside diameter reduces bending strain, lowers deformation memory, and helps protect flatness after unrolling. This is why minimum inside diameter should be controlled as a process condition, not treated as arbitrary packing preference.

Notes and assumptions

This page uses the standard surface bending-strain relation ε = t / (2R) as a simplified engineering model. It is useful for explaining trend and risk, but the real wrinkle threshold also depends on winding tension, storage time, temperature, moisture, layer stiffness, and coating hardness.

So the calculation should be presented as a practical decision tool: smaller inside diameter means higher strain and higher wrinkle risk. Exact acceptable limits should still be validated by trial rolls and flattening tests on the real production material.
